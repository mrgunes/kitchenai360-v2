Every major kitchen appliance comes with a specification sheet — a technical document from the manufacturer that lists dimensions, clearance requirements, utility connections, and installation notes. These documents are the source of truth for kitchen planning, and knowing how to read them accurately can prevent a significant category of construction errors.

## Where to Find Spec Sheets

Manufacturer websites publish spec sheets for every current model, typically as downloadable PDFs. Search for the model number plus "spec sheet" or "installation guide." Most major appliance brands also maintain product pages with a "Documents" or "Specs & Downloads" tab.

For discontinued models, the manufacturer may still host the document, or it may be available through appliance parts databases and dealer archives.

**Always use the spec sheet for the specific model you have selected, not a similar model.** Dimensions and rough-in requirements vary meaningfully between product lines and even between model years.

## Key Dimensions to Extract

### Overall Dimensions

Spec sheets list height, width, and depth. These are not always what you expect.

- **Height** is often given with and without adjustable feet or handles. Confirm which measurement applies to your installation context.
- **Width** is typically the cabinet cutout or rough opening width, not just the appliance body. Verify whether the spec sheet lists the appliance width or the opening width.
- **Depth** may or may not include the door. Many spec sheets list depth with and without the door open. For layout planning, you need both: depth with door closed (for clearance to adjacent elements) and door-open depth (for floor space clearance in front).

### Rough Opening vs. Installed Dimensions

For built-in and semi-integrated appliances, spec sheets distinguish between:

- **Rough opening**: the cabinet cutout or framed opening size
- **Installed dimensions**: the appliance size after installation

These can differ by a few inches. Use the rough opening dimensions when sizing cabinetry, and the installed dimensions when planning adjacent clearances.

### Clearance Requirements

Most spec sheets specify minimum clearances:

- Side clearances (often fractions of an inch for ventilation)
- Top clearance (especially for refrigerators)
- Front clearance (space needed for service access)

These are in addition to the clearances you will maintain for functional use. A refrigerator may require 1 inch on each side for ventilation — that is separate from the 15 inches of landing counter space you need on the handle side.

## Utility Connection Information

Spec sheets specify all utility connections the appliance requires:

### Electrical

- Voltage and amperage (e.g., 120V/15A, 240V/30A)
- Plug type or hardwire connection
- Dedicated circuit requirement (most ranges, dishwashers, and refrigerators require dedicated circuits)
- Location of the electrical connection (rear, left side, bottom)

This information goes directly to your electrician. Make sure the panel location and connection point are called out on your electrical plan.

### Plumbing

For dishwashers and refrigerators with ice makers:

- Supply line location and size
- Drain connection location (dishwashers)
- Water pressure range

The location of the supply and drain connections affects where those lines need to be roughed in — which may differ from where you assume they would be.

### Gas

For gas ranges and cooktops:

- BTU ratings per burner and overall
- Gas connection location and size
- Flexible connector requirements

## Ventilation and Hood Pairing

Range and cooktop spec sheets often specify:

- Minimum CFM (cubic feet per minute) for compatible hood ventilation
- Clearance to combustible overhead cabinets
- Recommended hood height above cooking surface

Cross-reference this with the hood spec sheet to confirm compatibility. A high-output range paired with an undersized hood will not ventilate adequately regardless of how it is installed.

## A Practical Workflow

1. Finalize the appliance selections (make, model, finish) before dimensioning cabinets
2. Download the spec sheet for each selected model
3. Extract and document: rough opening dimensions, clearance requirements, and utility connection locations
4. Transfer these to your floor plan and elevations as explicit annotations
5. Share the spec sheets directly with your cabinet fabricator and GC — do not rely on summaries

The spec sheet is the document that closes the gap between a floor plan and a set of cabinets that actually fit the appliances you ordered.
