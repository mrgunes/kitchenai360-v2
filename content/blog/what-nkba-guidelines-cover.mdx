The National Kitchen and Bath Association publishes a set of kitchen design guidelines that many designers treat as the baseline standard for residential kitchen planning. But the guidelines are often referenced without much explanation of what they actually say or how they fit alongside local building codes.

Here is a plain-language overview.

## What the Guidelines Are

The NKBA kitchen design guidelines are a set of 31 recommended practices covering clearances, traffic flow, countertop space, storage, lighting, and appliance placement. They are described as guidelines — not code — meaning compliance is recommended but not legally required.

That distinction matters. Building codes are enforceable law. NKBA guidelines are industry-developed best practices intended to represent quality design.

In practice, many designers and contractors treat the guidelines as a quality baseline and use them alongside local codes when planning projects.

## What They Cover

The 31 guidelines address several overlapping areas:

**Walkways and clearances.** Guidelines specify minimum widths for kitchen walkways (at least 36 inches for a one-cook kitchen, 42 inches for two cooks), clearance in front of appliances, and passage space around islands and peninsulas.

**Work triangle and work zones.** The classic work triangle gets refined into a broader concept of work zones. Guidelines address the relationship between preparation, cooking, cleanup, and storage areas.

**Countertop landing areas.** Appliances like dishwashers, refrigerators, and microwaves each have associated guidelines for nearby countertop space. A microwave, for example, should have counter space adjacent to it for safe dish handling.

**Storage.** Guidelines address the total volume of accessible storage, placement of frequently used items within reach, and considerations for accessibility.

**Sink and dishwasher placement.** Specific guidelines address sink position relative to windows and adjacent counter space, and dishwasher placement relative to the sink.

**Lighting.** The guidelines address general illumination, task lighting over work surfaces, and light source placement relative to cabinets.

**Accessibility.** A subset of guidelines addresses layouts that accommodate a wider range of physical abilities, including knee clearances under countertops and accessible storage heights.

## What the Guidelines Do Not Cover

The NKBA guidelines do not cover structural elements, plumbing specifications, electrical code compliance, or ventilation requirements. Those are governed by local building codes, which vary by jurisdiction.

They also do not address finish specifications, material quality, appliance brands, or budget. The guidelines focus entirely on spatial and ergonomic planning.

## How They Relate to Building Codes

Building codes — typically based on the International Residential Code with local amendments — specify what is legally required. Electrical work must meet NEC requirements. Plumbing must meet state and local codes. These are non-negotiable.

NKBA guidelines operate alongside this, describing what constitutes a well-designed kitchen within those code constraints. A kitchen can be fully code-compliant while still having a poorly designed work triangle or insufficient landing space. The guidelines address the design quality that codes do not.

## Using Guidelines in Practice

Designers typically check a project against both the applicable building codes and the NKBA guidelines during the planning phase. Where the two conflict, code requirements take precedence. Where guidelines suggest more generous clearances than code requires, following the guidelines generally produces a better-functioning kitchen.

Documenting which guidelines were reviewed — and noting any deliberate departures with client acknowledgment — is also common practice. This creates a record that the design was evaluated against an established standard.
